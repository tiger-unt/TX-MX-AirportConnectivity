# Introduction

This document reviews airports, airport activities, and air transportation in the Texas-Mexico border region. This review is part of a larger project that is tasked with (a) assessing the role of airports in facilitating U.S./Texas-Mexico trade and travel, (b) assessing the Texas-Mexico border region’s air passenger and air freight connectivity with the remainder of Texas and Mexico, and (c) documenting the relationships among airports and local economies in the border region. This document summarizes the study team’s progress to date on the first component of the study:  the literature review.

## 1.1 Significance of the Work

The Texas-Mexico border is 1,254-miles long with 34 operational international bridges and border crossings within twelve ports of entry (POEs) that process pedestrians, passenger vehicles, and freight (rail and commercial trucks). According to U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP) data, 17.5 million pedestrians, 35.1 million passenger vehicles, and 5.4 million commercial trucks crossed into Texas from Mexico in 2024 through Texas’ land ports of entry. In 2024, an estimated $553 billion of freight flowed through the Texas border, representing nearly all Texas-Mexico trade and 66 percent of all U.S.-Mexico trade. 
The border region also contains many cities whose economies depend on cross-border trade. For example, maquiladoras in Mexico leverage low labor costs and preferential tariffs to assemble imported components into finished goods such as electronics and automotive parts. Other specialist industries include agriculture, logistics, and technical services. The border attracts many of these industries due to efficient transportation networks, duty exemptions, and skilled and cost-effective labor. However, in turn, these industries are also essential for ensuring cost effective goods and services throughout Texas and the rest of the United States.
While the majority of Texas’ cross-border passenger and freight movement occurs at international bridges and border crossings, the border region also includes several airports. The role of border airports in facilitating cross-border trade and travel has been less well studied and is consequently less well understood than land POEs. However, such knowledge is important for several reasons:
- Air is an important mode in facilitating the movement of people (both for leisure and business) between Texas and Mexico,
- Air is a critical modal option for high-value, time sensitive supply chains that have adopted a just-in-time business model, and
- Planning for and prioritizing connectivity projects to airports require an understanding of both the role and importance of airports in facilitating U.S./Texas-Mexico trade and travel.

# Background Information and Definitions

This section of the document provides background information and defines airport codes, air services, airport types, and airport runways. In addition to their value for understanding airport and air carrier operations, these definitions are essential for understanding how air transportation activities are reported (for example, to the Bureau of Transportation Statistics, as described in a later section).

## 2.1. Airport Codes


### International Air Transport Association (IATA)

An International Air Transport Association (IATA) airport code is a unique three-letter code that designates airports, cities (served by one or more airports), and other infrastructure such as railway stations. IATA also publishes airline codes. IATA codes are the most familiar airport naming convention because they are used on baggage labels and in airline ticketing.
IATA is an airline trade association that has several focus areas, including safety, business, environment, hazardous waste transportation, and training. As part of its regular operations, IATA publishes semi-annual updates to airport codes. There are 17,576 possible three-letter IATA codes. This means that some smaller airports may not be assigned an IATA code. In fact, IATA codes are generally only assigned to airports with scheduled flights. IATA codes may also be reassigned occasionally in line with airport activities and business.

### International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO)

The International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) is a United Nations agency that coordinates international air traffic. The ICAO adopts standards and recommended practices concerning air navigation, infrastructure, flight inspection, and cross-border procedures for international civil aviation. Part of ICAO's regular operations is to assign unique codes to airports. This standard coding convention enables tools such as navigation charts, Global Positioning Systems (GPS), and weather reports to work together across international boundaries. The codes also help reduce errors and misunderstandings in critical flight operations data.  
ICAO airport codes are always four letters. Although there are no strictly enforced coding rules, the first letter of the ICAO code denotes the airport's general geographic region. These include ‘E’ for Northern Europe, ‘S’ for South America, ‘C’ for Canada, and ‘K’ for the contiguous United States. The second letter typically narrows an airport down to a country or region, and the last two letters identify the airport. However, for U.S. airports in the contiguous United States, the convention is to append a K to the beginning of a three-digit Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) code. Mexican airports are all assigned the ‘MM’ as the first two letters. 
ICAO codes are never reused. Therefore, airfields that have since closed may still be assigned an ICAO code (note that this does not apply to airports that are renamed).
Most large public airports are assigned an ICAO code. In countries with a relatively small number of airports, almost all airports have an ICAO code. However, limits on code availability mean that, in some countries with large airport systems, many airports cannot be assigned an international code. In such cases, countries may issue their own pseudo-ICAO codes that follow a consistent coding scheme but are not officially used by the ICAO, and others, such as the United States, Mexico, and Russia, define local codes using their own coding conventions (often called regional or Location Identifier (LID) codes).
There is no official central database of ICAO codes. Instead, codes are published in the document “ICAO Document 7910: Location Indicators” and are updated every 28 to 56 days in accordance with ICAO's regular update cycle. While the publication is not free, other organizations use these updated publications to maintain their own databases and Application Programming Interfaces (APIs).
The ICAO also maintains a list of unique three-letter airline codes. The designators are listed in ICAO Document 8585: Designators for Aircraft Operating Agencies, Aeronautical Authorities and Services.

### Federal Aviation Administration (FAA)

The Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) has developed its own coding system for airports in the United States only. Every registered airport in the United States is assigned an alphanumeric FAA LID (Location Identifier). Most major airports are assigned a three-letter code, for example, SFO for San Francisco. Minor airfields are often assigned a mix of three or four alphanumeric characters, while private airfields are typically assigned a four-digit alphanumeric code.
Special entities reserve some codes, for example, the U.S. Department of the Navy reserves all codes beginning with N for its airfields, while Canada’s aviation authority reserves codes beginning with Y. FAA codes are designed to be somewhat compatible with ICAO codes. For example, the ICAO code for most U.S. airports is usually the letter K followed by the FAA code. However, since ICAO codes are limited to airfields with an airline service (or recent service), not all FAA codes have an equivalent ICAO code. 

### Mexican Airports

The Mexico Air Transportation Authority is the Federal Civil Aviation Agency (AFAC), a division of the Secretariat of Infrastructure, Communications and Transportation (SICT). AFAC was preceded (prior to 2019) by the Directorate General of Civil Aeronautics (Dirección General de Aeronáutica Civil, DGAC). Mexico has a dense network of public and private airports and airfields such that not all of them can be assigned an international code (i.e., ICAO or IATA). Therefore, AFAC issues ‘local’ codes that help identify all federally registered airports. 

## 2.2 Commercial vs General Aviation Airports and Activities

The ICAO defines General Aviation (GA) as all civil aviation aircraft operations except for commercial air transport or aerial work. Aerial work is defined as specialized aviation services, including pilot training, agricultural flights, banner towing, firefighting, medical evacuation, and search and rescue. Examples of GA include corporate aviation, business aviation, personal or private travel, air tourism, and recreational flying.
The FAA defines General Aviation Airports as public-use airports that do not have scheduled service or have less than 2,500 annual passenger boardings (49 USC 47102(8)). 
In contrast, Commercial Activities involve an aircraft operation that transports people, cargo, or mail for payment or hire. Commercial air transport includes scheduled and charter flights for passengers and cargo (defined later). Commercial aviation activities in the United States require, at a minimum, a commercial pilot license and an airline transport pilot license (ATPL).

## 2.3 Scheduled vs Chartered Commercial Air Services

Federal code distinguishes between scheduled and chartered commercial flight services. This distinction affects the operation of commercial airline service companies as follows:
**Scheduled Services:** According to 14 CFR Part 119, airlines operating scheduled passenger and cargo flights must file a service schedule with the governing transportation agency. A scheduled operation is a type of air transportation service in which an air carrier or commercial operator provides passenger flights for compensation or hire. In this context, the operator must publicly announce key details, including the departure location, departure time, and arrival location, in advance. This definition excludes operations classified as public charter services, which are subject to different regulations.
**Charter Services:** A charter flight is a service contracted, typically on a non-scheduled basis, in which the charterer assumes greater control over the aircraft's operation, while the aircraft is legally owned or operated by a licensed third-party operator. The U.S. Department of Transportation’s (DOT’s) public charter regulations (14 CFR Part 380) require all companies that wish to arrange public charter flights to submit and obtain DOT acceptance of a charter prospectus. A charter prospectus comprises a set of documents in which the public charter operator demonstrates that it has taken the steps required by DOT’s public charter regulations. A charter prospectus also includes a flight schedule that may cover a single charter flight or a series of charter flights for up to one year. For example, a flight carrying cargo or passengers from location A to B would be considered a private flight if the aircraft is operated by the same company as the cargo or passengers, or a charter flight if conducted in a plane contracted by the company and operated by a licensed operator. 

## 2.4 U.S. Airport Types


### U.S. Federal Code definitions

The Federal Code also defines several types of airports based on their role, particularly the presence of Customs and Border Protection (CBP) facilities required to process international flights. The following definitions are of note:
- **International Airports: **The U.S. Code of Federal Regulations (CFR) defines an international airport as any airport designated by:
  - The Secretary of the Treasury or the Commissioner of Customs as a port of entry for aircraft arriving in the United States from any place outside thereof and for the merchandise carried on such aircraft;
  - The Attorney General as a port of entry for aliens arriving on such aircraft; and
  - The Secretary of Health and Human Services as a place for quarantine inspection.
Note that this definition is distinct from the ‘intuitive’ understanding of the meaning of an international airport (i.e., an airport that conducts regular international flights). The study team infers that the codified definition ensures a strategic placement of airports with CBP facilities.
- **User Fee Airport:** An airport where the airport pays for CBP presence and facilities. This enables the airport to handle international flights. CBP costs are typically passed on to airport users. 
- **Landing Rights Airport:** Any airport, other than an international airport or user-free airport, at which flights from a foreign area are permitted to land by CBP.

### National Plan of Integrated Airport Systems

The National Plan of Integrated Airport Systems (NPIAS) is a federally mandated document (49 U.S.C. § 47103) that outlines a plan for the development of U.S. public-use airports. The plan is prepared by the Secretary of Transportation (Federal Aviation Administration [FAA]) and details the types and estimated costs of eligible airport development necessary to provide a safe, efficient, and integrated system of public-use airports. 
The FAA uses a variety of data (e.g., aviation-related activity, ownership, aeronautical functions serving the public interest, etc.) to determine the appropriate category and hub/role of each NPIAS airport. Airports meeting the commercial service requirements (i.e., public airports with more than 2,500 enplanements a year and scheduled service) are updated every year, while all other airports are updated every other year.

### Primary Airports vs Non-Primary Airports

**Primary airports** are defined in 49 U.S.C. § 47102(16) as public airports receiving scheduled air carrier service with 10,000 or more enplaned passengers per year. The statute further groups primary airports into four sub-categories based on each airport’s enplanement totals:
- *Large Hubs:* Large hubs are defined by statute as commercial service airports that each account for one percent or more of total U.S. passenger enplanements.
- *Medium Hubs:* Medium hubs are defined by statute as commercial service airports that each account for between 0.25 percent and one percent of total U.S. passenger enplanements.
- *Small Hubs:* Small hubs are defined by statute as commercial service airports that account for 0.05 percent to 0.25 percent of total U.S. passengers.
- *Non**-**hub Primary:* Non-hub primary airports are defined by statute as commercial service airports that account for less than 0.05 percent of all commercial passenger enplanements but have more than 10,000 annual enplanements.
**Non-primary airports** include commercial service airports (i.e., public airports receiving scheduled passenger service and between 2,500 and 9,999 enplaned passengers per year), general aviation airports, and reliever airports. These airports primarily support general aviation aircraft. Non-primary airports are grouped into five categories based on existing activity (e.g., the number and types of based aircraft and the volume and types of flights), geographic factors, and public-interest functions. The classifications are as follows:
- *National:* National airports are located in metropolitan areas near major business centers and support flying throughout the nation and the world. These airports provide pilots with attractive alternatives to primary airports.
- *Regional: *Regional airports are also in metropolitan areas and serve relatively large populations. These airports support regional economies with interstate and some long-distance flying and have high levels of activity, including some jets and multi-engine propeller aircraft.
- *Local:* Local airports are located near larger population centers but not necessarily in metropolitan areas. They are a critical component of the U.S. general aviation system, providing communities with access to local and regional markets. Typically, local airports are located near larger population centers but not necessarily in metropolitan areas. 
- *Basic: *Basic airports are community airports, providing a means for private general aviation flying, linking a community with the national airport system.
- *Unclassified:* Unclassified airports tend to have limited activity.
- *New Airports:* Airports under construction at the time of the National Aeronautical Information Publication (NAIP).

## 2.5	Airport Facilities


### Airport Runways

Airport runways are represented by two numbers, for example, 18/36. The numbers represent the magnetic heading rounded to the nearest 10 degrees and divided by 10. So, a runway named 18/36 indicates a single runway that can be approached from two directions: either 180° or 360°. 
Airports with multiple runways that have the same magnetic heading (i.e., are parallel to each other) have a letter appended to the number to differentiate them, such as "L" (left), "C" (center), or "R" (right). For example, L18/R36 denotes the left-most runway of a pair viewed from a heading of approximately 180 degrees or the right-most runway when approaching from the opposite direction.

Deadheading is likely to influence transportation costs and may be an essential factor to consider when assessing the cost and benefits of air versus other modes of transportation.
Finally, Kentucky, California, Arkansas, Tennessee, and Florida rank higher than Texas in terms of air cargo weight handled in 2024. This may be partially because their airports are geographically distant from the border and major Mexican cities, making air transportation more viable relative to other modes, such as trucking.  Modeling the operational efficiency of air transportation versus other modes will help determine the importance of Texas’ airports (and specifically border airports) in facilitating trade with Mexico.

## 4.4	Trend in Texas-Mexico Air Trade and Travel Transportation

Figure 2 shows the annual trend in Texas-Mexico air trade and travel activity for Texas’ border airports and four major non-border airports for context. Each graph panel (row) shows annual Texas imports (blue line) and exports (red line) of air cargo (left column) and annual Texas arrivals (blue line) and departures (red line) of air passengers (right column) from and to Mexico from 2000 to 2024. Figure x shows that Texas’ major airports (i.e., Dallas/Fort Worth International airport [DFW], George Bush Intercontinental airport [IAH], San Antonio International airport [SAT], and Austin-Bergstrom International airport [AUS]) show consistent increases in Texas-Mexico air passenger travel between 2000 and 2024. The figure shows no long-term trends in Texas-Mexico air passenger travel at Texas’ border airports. Instead, Texas’ border airports exhibit inconsistent peaks and plateaus over the analysis period, including periods in which departures exceed arrivals and vice versa. Also, at most airports and particularly at the major airports, passenger arrivals and departures are approximately equal each year. 
Figure 2 also shows that Texas’ major airports show distinct drops in Texas-Mexico air passenger travel during 2020; most likely caused by the COVID-19 pandemic. In contrast, some border airports (i.e., Brownsville South Padre Island International Airport [BRO], El Paso International Airport [ELP], and Laredo International Airport [LRD]) show a distinct peak in passenger departures during 2020. This peak may be explained by the fact that U.S.-Mexico land border crossings were closed to non-essential travel from March 2020 to November 8, 2021 to slow the spread of COVID-19. This restriction did not apply to air travel and can therefore explain the increase in Texas-Mexico air passenger travel (specifically departures to Mexico) at Texas’ border airports.
In contrast to air passenger travel, there are no easily identifiable trends in air cargo imports and exports from and to Mexico at Texas airports. As identified in previous analyses, cargo imports and exports are typically unbalanced at all airports and across all years of analysis. At Texas’ border airports, considerable variation in air cargo occurred from year to year. For example, Valley International Airport (HRL) experienced an approximately 10-year period of high cargo imports and exports (in terms of weight), but then a return to a much lower baseline level. Similarly, Del Rio International Airport (DRT) and Brownsville South Padre Island International Airport (BRO) experienced several years in which air cargo imports and exports were temporarily elevated, only to return to baseline levels.

## 5.1 Air Passenger Customs Processes


### Mexico to U.S. - Passenger Entry Processes

Mexican citizens need a visa to enter the U.S. The type of visa depends on the trip purpose (e.g., tourism, business, or school). Visa types include business visa (B-1), tourism visa (B-2), combination business/tourism visa (B-1/B-2), student visa (F), exchange students/visitors (J), temporary worker visas (so called nonimmigrant NAFTA professional visa or TN visa), and intra-company transfer visas (L). The steps involved for Mexican citizens that want to obtain a non-immigrant visa to enter the U.S. are:
- Mexican citizens need to apply for a non-immigrant visa. This step involves completing and submitting the Non-Immigrant Visa Application (DS-160) form online at the Consular Electronic Application Center (CEAC) website and payment of the visa application fee.  
- Applicants need to gather required document, including a passport that is valid for six months beyond the applicant’s intended stay, passport-sized photo, and proof of ties to Mexico (e.g., property ownership, employment, or family ties), and evidence of financial resources to pay for the travel.
- Applicants schedule an appointment for a visa interview at a U.S. Embassy or Consulate in Mexico.
- Applicant needs to bring all required documentation, DS-160 confirmation page, and visa fee receipt to visa interview. U.S. Embassy or Consulate staff will ask questions about the trip purpose, ties to Mexico, and collect biometric data during the visa interview.
- If approved, the required visa will be stamped in the applicant’s passport and the passport will be returned to the applicant. If denied, the applicant will receive a letter with an explanation for the denial., 
Mexican citizens or residents that want to travel to the U.S. border zones for short trips also have the option to apply for a Border Crossing Card (BCC). The BCC (also known as a laser visa) functions as a Border Crossing Card and combined B-1/B-2 visa. The BCC is valid for 10 years and can be used at all land, air, and seaports within border zones (i.e., 25 miles of the border within Texas). To apply for the BCC:
- Mexican citizens or residents need to complete form DSP-150 online and pay the application fee. Applicants need to demonstrate strong ties with Mexico. Requirements for the BCC are codified in 8 CFR 212.1., 
- xx interview.
- xx biometric data.
Upon arriving at the airport, Mexican citizens and residents need to present a valid passport and visa or BCC (if within the border zone) to Customs and Border Protection (CBP) officials at the first inspection station (primary inspection). CBP staff will scan the documents for verification purposes, ask questions about the purpose of travel, and record biometric data (i.e., fingerprints and digital photo). This screening can also be conducted at an automated terminal. The CBP officer will decide whether the traveler is clear to enter the United States or if the traveler needs to be directed to secondary inspection – a more detailed and intensive inspection. During secondary inspection:
- The traveler could be asked more detailed questions about the purpose of the trip, finances, U.S. contacts, etc.; 
- Submitted documents may be scrutinized and compared against databases; 
- Carry-on bags and luggage may be searched, and 
- Electronic devices (e.g., laptops, and cell phones) may be inspected.
Secondary inspection is a much more involved process and may take several hours. At the conclusion of secondary inspection, the CBP officer may clear the traveler to enter the United States (after all concerns have been addressed); some items in carry-on bags and luggage may be confiscated or fines imposed if unauthorized or not declared; or entry into the United States may be denied. The latter usually occurs when employment is suspected but the traveler does not have a work visa, insufficient travel funds, security risks or prior violations. Finally, visas may be canceled prohibiting the traveler from entering the United States.

### U.S. to Mexico - Passenger Entry Processes

U.S. citizens traveling to Mexico does not require a visa if the trip purpose is tourism, business or transit and up to 180 days, but the following requirements must be met:
- U.S. citizens require a valid U.S. passport book when traveling by air to Mexico. The passport must be valid when the traveler enters and leave Mexico.
- U.S. citizens require a FMM (Forma Migratoria Múltiple) tourist card to enter Mexico. The cost of the FMM is included in the airline ticket price. The airline will provide the traveler with a paper FMM to complete when the traveler boards the international flight to Mexico. The FMM states how long the traveler can stay in Mexico. The paper-based FMM is, however, being phased out at major international airports.
- U.S. citizens must that are not residents of Mexico must present a return or outbound air ticket.
- U.S. citizens must complete a Mexican customs declaration form that lists all items that the traveler is brining into the country. The customs declaration form can be completed online.
Upon arrival at the Mexican airport, the traveler needs to present a valid passport and FMM tourist card (at airports where FMM tourist cards are still required). The Mexican immigration officer will stamp the passport and two portions of the FMM. One portion of the FMM card will be returned to the traveler.  At major international airports where FMM tourist cards are not required, an immigration agent will stamp a valid passport, note that the traveler is a “Turista” and the number of days the traveler is allowed to stay in Mexico.
After immigration, the traveler must proceed to collect luggage. Mexico uses a green light/red light system.  The traveler must press a button. If the light turns green, the traveler can enter Mexico without being inspected. However, customs officers can still direct travelers that had a green light to be inspected. If the light turns red, the traveler’s bags will be inspected by customs officers. 
U.S. citizens require a visa for employment, volunteering or internships, study programs, residency, and stays longer than 180 days. Visa applications are done through the Mexican consulate or embassy in the U.S. It involves the submittal of an application/documentation (including copy of valid passport, passport photos, proof of income or employment, school enrollment), an interview at the consulate/embassy, and the payment of a visa fee. 

## 5.2	Freight Processes


### U.S. Customs Clearance Process:  Air Cargo Imports to U.S.

The U.S. customs clearance process for importing goods by air into the country involves a number of steps:
- Buyer or seller of imported goods contracts with freight forwarder or logistics provider to make transportation arrangement for cargo.
- Air Carrier or another entity that meets the CBP requirements for filing must submit Air Cargo Advance Screening (ACAS) data for any inbound aircraft that transports commercial cargo through a CBP-approved electronic data interchange system prior to the air cargo being loaded on the aircraft. In addition, if food/animal feed are imported from Mexico into the U.S., prior notice needs to be filed with the Food and Drug Administration (FDA).
- CBP reviews the ACAS data, conducts a risk assessment of the ACAS filing, and makes a referral:
  - **Referral for information** in the case of non-descriptive, inaccurate, or insufficient information.
  - **Referral for screening **if the assessed risk is high enough for the shipment to require requires to be resolved according to a TSA-approved enhanced screening method. 
Referrals need to be resolved prior to airplane’s departure.
- Upon the airplane arriving at the U.S. airport, CBP officers and relevant Government Agencies conduct primary inspection of all air cargo shipments.
  - **Primary inspection** involves review of electronic manifest and advance air cargo data submitted through ACAS; verification of documentation (Air Way Bill, commercial invoice, certificate of origin), assessment of declared value, HS code, and country of origin, and initial screening of compliance with U.S. import regulations. 
  - **Secondary inspection** involves detailed examination of documents and packaging, physical inspection of cargo, sampling and laboratory analysis (of certain products such as food and chemicals), consultation with the appropriate agencies for regulated goods, and the verification of labeling, certifications (e.g., for agricultural products), and prior notice filings (e.g., for food shipments).
- CBP and other Government Agencies resolve any holds, discrepancies or regulatory issues.
- Cargo shipment is released for collection by importer for distribution into the U.S.

### U.S. Customs Clearance Process:  Air Cargo Exports from U.S.

The U.S. customs clearance process for exporting goods by air from the country involves a number of steps:
- The exporter identifies the Schedule B number for the commodity to be exported. The Schedule B number must be entered into the Automated Export System (AES). 
- The exporter or its agent (i.e., Air Freight Forwarder) completes and files the Electronic Export Information (EEI) in the Automated Export System (AES) when the value of the commodity classified under each individual Schedule B number is over $2,500, or if there exists a mandatory filing requirement, i.e., an export license is required.   The EEI includes information about the shipment (e.g., transportation mode, airport of export, departure date, origin state, country of destination, whether hazardous product), exporter and importer information, export commodity information (e.g., Schedule B commodity code, commodity description, shipment value, shipment weight), and carrier information (e.g., carrier code, carrier name). Export permits or licenses are required for the export of certain regulated goods (e.g., agricultural products or pharmaceuticals) and needs to be obtained from the relevant government agency.
- If the EEI filing is accepted, the exporter or its agent (i.e., Air Freight Forwarder) will receive an Internal Transaction Number (ITN). If the EEI filing is rejected, the exporter or its agent will receive an email with a rejection statement.
- The exporter or Air Freight Forwarder prepares the Air Waybill and supporting documentation (e.g., commercial invoice, packing list, and any certificates or permits). The Air Waybill includes accurate information about the commodity, exporter, and importer. The ITN is also included on the first page of the Air Waybill. 
- The exporter or Air Freight Forwarder schedules the collection and delivery of air export shipment to the air carrier. Qualified personnel inspect cargo and verify Air Waybill for accuracy before submitting and delivery to airline.
- Airline staff verify Air Waybill and cargo shipment before signing acceptance confirmations. Federal regulations require that all air cargo must be screened for explosives to ensure safety. Airline also checks whether air cargo has been screened.
- Airline submits all required documents to CBP, who may (a) review the EEI, (b) inspect cargo, and or (c) verify all permits and license requirements. CBP informs airline that shipment is cleared and can be loaded on the aircraft.
- Airline loads shipment on aircraft.

#### Innovative Customs Processing:  Laredo International Airport

The Laredo International Airport (LRD) implemented the Unified Cargo Processing (UCP) program at its Federal Inspection Station in 2013.  The Federal Inspection Station houses both U.S. Customs and Border Patrol (CBP) and Mexican customs officials (Servicio de Administración Tributaria, or SAT), who jointly perform inspections on U.S. exports destined for Mexico. Air cargo inspected jointly at LRD, have expedited clearance at airports in eight Mexican cities. In other words, these air cargo shipments can be released directly to the importer upon arrival at the airports without having to go through the Mexican customs process. When UCP was implemented at LRD, the program was limited to exports in the automotive, aerospace, and electronics industries.
The trade benefits of UCP include streamlined customs procedures, avoiding production delays and reducing wait times (critical for just-in-time deliveries), enhancing national security by streamlining the supply chain, and maximizing limited resources. ,* *
CBP reported the following benefits from UCP for importers:
- streamlined cargo movement,
- prioritizing cargo shipments that require an intensive exam (i.e., shipments that require unloading the entire shipment for a complete examination/inspection and reloading),
- joint inspection of cargo shipments that require an intensive exam (i.e., shipment only needs to be unloaded once), and a 
- reduction in transit inventory costs with the implementation of UCP.

### Mexican Customs Clearance Process:  Air Cargo Imports to Mexico

Mexico’s customs clearance process for importing goods into the country involves several steps:
- The importer needs to register with the Mexican Registry of Importers (“Padrón de Importadores”). The step involves submitting documentation to the Mexican Tax Administration (SAT) to establish the company’s eligibility and compliance with local regulations for importing the specified goods.
- The importer needs to classify the imported goods using the Harmonized System (HS) codes to ensure compliance with Mexican customs regulations, the accurate calculation of duties and taxes (and to prevent delays and fines).
- The importer is required (i.e., it is mandatory) to hire a licensed customs broker (agente aduanal) for commercial imports,  to assist the importer with documentation, cargo classification, and compliance with Mexico’s customs regulations.
- The licensed customs broker prepares the Import Declaration (Pedimento de Importación), which specifies the import shipment’s weight/volume, value, origin, destination, and HS code. The licensed customs broker generates the Documento de Operación para Despacho Aduanero) (DODA) online using the SAT platform. The DODA is a mandatory digital form that needs to be submitted before imports are presented for customs clearance at Mexican airports. The DODA contains the Pedimento number, carrier and transport details, product description, the electronic signature of the customs broker, a digital stamp of SAT, and a digital QR code that customs scans. The DODA ensures that all documentation is linked digitally, improves transparency, and streamlines customs inspections.
- Mexican importer or broker uploads the Import Declaration (Pedimento) and supporting documentation to the customs electronic system for review and pay all applicable duties, taxes, and fees.
- The xxxxx presents the DODA to Mexican customs. Mexican customs scans the DODA’s QR code with the SAT application to activate the Automated Selection Mechanism. The DODA removes the need to show the “Relación de documentos”, the printout of the “Pedimentos”, the “Formato para la Impresión Simplificada del Pedimento”, the “Aviso consolidado”, the Pedimento Parte II, and or the “Copia simple”. 
- The Automated Selection Mechanism is used to determine if the shipment is released with document review only (“green” light) or if additional physical inspection is required (red light). 
  - If only the documents are reviewed, Customs officials review the submitted Import Declaration (Pedimento) and supporting documentation for accuracy, completeness, compliance, and consistency of the Pedimento, DODA, and supporting documentation.
  - If Customs officials physically inspect the goods, Customs officials will visually verify that the contents and packaging are consistent with documentation provided (e.g., quantities and product descriptions) and complies with regulations.
  - Customs officials release goods for delivery to final destination.

### Mexican Customs Clearance Process:  Air Cargo Exports from Mexico

Mexico’s customs clearance process for exporting goods from Mexico to the U.S./Texas involves several steps:
- The Mexican exporter needs to register with the Mexican Exporters Registry, managed by the Mexican Tax Administration (SAT). Registering with the Exporters Registry requires submittal of an application online through the SAT portal, along with required documentation, including an active Registro Federal de Contribuyentes (RFC)/Federal Taxpayers Registry code and a compliance certificate.
- The Mexican exporter needs to determine if the goods require any special permits, certificates or compliance with United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement (USMCA).
- The Mexican exporter prepares/obtains all required supporting documentation (e.g., commercial invoice, packing list, Air Way Bill, certificate of origin, and any necessary permits).
- The licensed Mexican Customs Broker prepares the official Mexican Export Declaration (Pedimento de Exportación) and generates the Documento de Operación para Despacho Aduanero) (DODA) online using the SAT platform.
  - The Mexican Customs Broker submits the Pedimento de Exportación electronically to xxxx. The Pedimento includes the HS Code, the value of the goods, tariff classification, the transportation details (including information about the transportation service provider) and proof of payment for any applicable duties or fees.
  - The DODA is a mandatory digital form that needs to be submitted before exports are presented for customs clearance at Mexican airports. 
- The Mexican Exporter/Customs Broker engages a freight forwarder for air freight services (air cargo booking) and ground transportation.
- The contracted transportation company transports the shipment to the certified air cargo terminal at the Mexican airport.
- Upon arrival at the air cargo terminal, the transportation company/air carrier presents the necessary documentation to Aduanas for customs clearance.
- Aduanas/SAT reviews documentation and, if shipment is selected via the Automated Selection Mechanism, conducts physical inspection.
- Xxxx obtains customs release and load cargo onto the aircraft.  
- Xxxx sends advance cargo data to the U.S. authorities.

# Summary

This Technical Memorandum summarizes the study team’s progress to date in identifying information and data that is relevant to the objectives of the Texas-Mexico Airport Connectivity Study. The Technical Memorandum summarizes the preliminary results from analyzing Bureau of Transportation Statistics (BTS) data, but it should be noted that the T-100 data likely contains considerably more information than has been reported in this document. Given the volume of data and the broad range of questions about border airports relevant to this project, the study team proposes compiling the data into an interactive dashboard. This would enable TxDOT and stakeholders to fully explore the data and reduce the burden of reporting multiple analyses in a traditional static report. Given that the T-100 data is regularly updated, a dashboard approach would also help keep the analyses and subsequent interpretations up to date.
Overall, the study team showed that the T-100 data indicate the following about airport activities in the border region:
- Although the absolute volume of cargo transported through Texas’ border airports is small, their value to local economies may still be relatively high. Given the uneven pattern of cargo movements over the years and the generally unequal balance of imports and exports, the study team proposes that many border airports serve as ‘emergency’ or ‘last-minute’ modal options for cross-border cargo transportation. This hypothesis will be explored through interviews with airport stakeholders (currently being scheduled). 
- It is also notable that CBP has a presence at many of Texas border airports due to the Federal Code, even though cross-border air cargo and passenger activities are relatively low and infrequent. 
- It is unfortunate that the BTS Transborder freight data (in its current form) does not provide much disaggregate insight into the types of commodities transported by air. However, if the value of border airports lies primarily as ‘emergency’ or ‘high value’ cargo transport options, then it is possible that a better approach to understanding their importance is by understanding the circumstances under which cargo is shipped via air, rather than the commodity per se. For example, while a two-digit commodity code might help identify industries that use air cargo (e.g., automotive), the code may not usefully specify the item being shipped (e.g., specific bolts or fasteners, or major body parts). More likely (assuming the hypothesis above), specific items (most likely high-value, low-bulk) are transported by air due to a particular supply chain issue or other temporary need. Interviews conducted during the next phase of this study may help confirm the ‘emergency supply’ hypothesis and help understand why certain commodities are occasionally shipped via air. Given the low cargo volumes at some border airports, it is possible that only a small number of local businesses account for most air cargo shipments.
To conclude, Texas ranks sixth in terms of Texas-Mexico air cargo compared to other U.S. states. This is despite the close ties between Texas' industries and Mexico's, and Texas’ large, developed (especially technology-oriented) economy. Although at first glance this may appear counterintuitive, it is possible that most cities in Texas are too close to Mexico for most types of air cargo to be economically viable. In other words, it is possible that most cross-border cargo can be transported overland at times and costs that outcompete air transportation in most cases (i.e., where cases are defined by commodity type and immediate value to supply chains or other essential functions). Clearly, border airports may be even more affected by this phenomenon. If this hypothesis is true, it may help to explain the sparse cargo activities at the border. 
This hypothesis of competition between surface-based and air-based cargo transport could be explored quantitatively and in greater detail in the next phases of the study. This approach, based on comparing the time and economic costs of surface vs. air transport, may provide a meaningful way to quantify the actual value of border airports. Such an approach would require additional details – for example, the time that surface cargo spends at the border (e.g., at drayage operations) versus the surface transportation and warehousing time for air cargo, delays and reliability, the cost of deadheading air cargo or surface freight, the identification of regions with industries that are most likely to support surface vs air cargo operations, etc. These questions will be explored in the subsequent phases of this project.

## A.1	Bureau of Transportation Statistics’ North American Transborder Freight Data

The Bureau of Transportation Statistics (BTS) TransBorder Freight database provides North American freight data by mode of transportation, commodity type, and geographic detail for U.S. exports to and imports from Canada and Mexico. BTS provides the data through a web-based ‘Mode, Port, and Commodity dashboard’ and raw data downloads in comma-delimited text format. BTS also provides monthly statistical summaries that highlight trends in the data.
BTS suggests that the TransBorder Freight data are *“…used for trade corridor studies, transportation infrastructure and border planning, marketing and logistics analyses, and other transportation research”.*  For this project, the study team is primarily interested in whether the data provides information on the type and quantities of air freight moving between Texas and Mexico. Specifically, the study team is interested in whether the data can provide high-resolution breakdowns of the origin and destination of freight and the types of commodities transported by air.

### Data Description

The TransBorder Freight data can be downloaded in month increments from https://www.bts.gov/topics/transborder-raw-data. Documentation for the data is also provided. Each month of data is contained within a *.zip file and consists of three *.csv files. The common feature of all files is that they contain fields describing the value (U.S. Dollar) and shipment weight (in Kilograms) of freight carried by different transportation modes from one specified location to another, either as exports or imports. The transportation modes accounted for in the data are Vessel, Air, Mail (U.S. Postal Service), Truck, Rail, Pipeline, Other, and Foreign Trade Zones (FTZs). Commodity types are classified according to the international standard Harmonized Commodity Description and Coding System or Harmonized System (HS) of tariff nomenclature.
According to the documentation, import and export data are provided at the spatial resolution of each Mexican state (or Canadian Province), and by a specified number of analysis areas within Texas (or more broadly, the U.S.). For example, Texas has 32 listed analysis areas:  U.S. Customs District and ports-of-entry included in the U.S. Customs District (see Table 20). Although spatial representations are not provided, deriving them independently is not technically challenging. Although not explicitly stated, the data appears to be reported at the port-of-entry when available, provided that doing so will not breach confidentiality procedures. The resolution of locations in Mexico is also variable. Although the data description suggests that export/import locations are provided at the resolution of each Mexican state, in practice, the database often reports them only at the country level.
The data consists of 3 files per month of data, named as follows, and containing the following data:
- **Dot1** – Data on the value, freight charges, and shipment weight of goods imported to or exported from specified U.S. states to specified Mexican or Canadian states/provinces. *This data file does not contain information on the commodity type.  *The data can be queried by the following fields: import/export, transportation mode, the origin of goods (domestic or foreign), and whether goods were transported using containers.
- **Dot2** - Data on the value and shipment weight of goods imported to or exported from specified U.S. states to specified Mexican or Canadian states/provinces. This data file further categorizes these values according to commodity type***. However, it does not break down locations into the **Customs District or **POE* shown in Table 19. Additionally, the data describe only the origin Mexican state for imports (the documentation states that the U.S. Customs documents on which the data are based do not ask for the destination Mexican state for exported goods). The data can be queried by the following fields: import/export, transportation mode, the origin of goods (domestic or foreign), and whether goods were transported using containers.
- **Dot3** - Data on the value and shipment weight of goods imported or exported to Mexico or Canada, processed at specific ports-of-entry (Table 19) and categorized by commodity types. *However, this data set does not provide information on the Mexican state to which goods are exported or from which goods are imported.* The data can be queried by the following fields: import/export, transportation mode, port of entry processed, the origin of goods (domestic or foreign), and whether goods were transported using containers.

## A.2 Bureau of Transportation Statistics T100 Air Carrier Statistics

BTS maintains a database of monthly air carrier statistics, including cargo and passenger flights, and scheduled and charter services. The data includes all domestic and international flights into or out of the U.S.
The BTS reports monthly flight data as Market and Segment summaries. These represent two distinct methods for measuring passenger or cargo movements, which are primarily concerned with flights that maintain the same ticket number but that contain multiple legs:
- **Market Data:** Passengers and cargo are recorded based on the number of passengers and freight on each leg of a multi-leg trip. 
- **Segment Data:** Passengers and cargo are recorded based on the number of passengers transported through each airport of a multi-leg flight.
Table 21 illustrates the difference between Market and Segment data for the following multi-leg flight example: 
*250 people take a flight from Houston Intercontinental to Laredo. At Laredo, 200 passengers deplane, and the other 50 passengers, along with 70 additional passengers, continue to Mexico City, where all passengers deplane.*
*Table 21. Market and Segment Calculation Example for a Multi-Leg Flight.*


| Trip Leg | Market | Segment |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Houston to Laredo | 200 | 250 |
| Laredo to Mexico City | 70 | 120 |
| Houston to Mexico City | 50 | No segment |

Typically, market data are used to report airline statistics because they provide the most intuitive measure of the number of passengers and cargo that are enplaned or deplaned at each airport. It can also be seen that segment data tends to overcount measures. 
The distinction between market and segment data is notable for several reasons:
- First, summaries obtained using the two methods may yield slightly different values and conclusions. 
- Second, the distinction facilitates the interpretation and integration of other airline data sources, such as flight data from Mexican providers. 
- Third, the BTS data contains different information for Segment versus Market data. For example, the Segment data details statistics such as the total number of flights, flight length, and flight delays that are not recorded in the Market data.

In addition, the database contains fields that describe each airport's country, its state or region, and the primary market (city or municipality) that it serves. Separate auxiliary tables provide the geographic coordinates of each airport, carrier information, and aircraft types registered with the FAA. These auxiliary tables reference the CARRIER_NAME, CARRIER_GROUP, AIRCRAFT GROUP, and other fields in the monthly activity summaries.
